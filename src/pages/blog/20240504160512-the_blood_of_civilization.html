The blood of civilization

Civilizations are organisms that are born, grow, wither, and die. Their cells are us, the humble human being. We group and form institutions and organizations (the organs). It is fun to think about the physiology of such a meta-creature. To map what we know about complex lifeforms at our scale onto a being that functions at a scale above. There are lots of places we could start but why not start with blood.

The blood of a civilization is money. Many characteristics of blood map well to how money functions. Let us consider a few.

  1. The blood of one organism is (normally) not compatible with

the blood of another. Broadly speaking, the local money of one civilization is not functional in another civilization without some kind of exchange.

  1. Blood carries vital nutrients to organs and cells

allowing their proper function. In a similar sense, the money of a civilization provides essential capital (cf nutrients) to organizations or individuals to perform some (hopefully) valuable function 1.

  1. Blood doesn't just diffuse around the body. It is carried in a

circulatory system. This tubular organ regulates the supply of oxygen and nutrients to the tissues that need it. Civilization has organs that operate similarly. Our system of banks is one such organ where money can be issued as debt to borrowers. Another is government, who can redistribute money via taxation (or inflation). Venture capital is another mechanism of allocating money to some valuable function. When an organ-ization with employees receives capital, it will distribute money to their employees (cells). This keeps the cells, organs, and the organism alive and healthy.

I have to introduce a caveat. Reasoning by analogy has hard limits. There is no reason the meta-creature of civilization has any resemblance to lower lifeforms (us). Our cells certainly don't look like us. What we can say is that cells regulate the distribution of nutrients and energy across the cell body. Higher-order life, such as us, also regulate the distribution of energy and nutrients. It seems likely that even higher-order organisms will also regulate the flow of nutrients and energy. Money represents a fundamental unit of 'energy' that civilization goes to great effort to distribute effectively.

With age, organisms develop diseases of the distribution system. Cells develop mitochondrial dysfunction and leaky organelles. Humans develop heart failure, atherosclerosis, and venous stasis. Civilizations develop problems with money and distribution.

The average lifespan of a civilization is 336 years. Leading causes of death include 'cardiovascular disease' and 'cancer.' Money is inflated, financial institutions are corrupted, wealth inequality increases beyond optimal, and cancers grow.

We have never had a truly global civilization. Modern Western democracy has come close, with the US dollar forming the monetary base. Followers of Ray Dalio with appreciate that the US dollar is losing its strength, and the global center of power is shifting. The rise of a new money signals a new dominant civilization.

Western money used to be gold, then it was fiat and a petrodollar. An intriguing new (in civilizational timespans), albeit controversial, option has emerged: bitcoin. As a monetary base, like gold, it solves the problem of inflation - a leading cause of civilizational mortality 2.

It is curious to think about what it would mean to run on a Bitcoin standard. It would represent the birth of a new civilization. Perhaps it's being born right now. But like all civilizations, it would still be susceptible to institutional corruption, cancers, and poor governance. The "bitcoin fixes this" meme is only true in the short term. All young civs are robust in the ways that matter.

Thinking about the physiology of civilization is a way of understanding when our society is diseased. Whether it's actually possible for a cell to "understand" the physiology of the organism it forms is another question entirely…

Footnotes:

1

The temptation here is to say that cells (people) or organs (companies) that accumulate a lot of wealth are inherently more good. "See, they're rich, they must be good." Continuing our analogy, malignant cells and tumours consume more resources than normal cells. People and companies can be rich by being useful OR by being cancerous. It is important to try and diagnose and treat the cancers, while letting the healthy tissue flourish.

2
